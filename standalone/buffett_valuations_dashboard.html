🛡 Strategic Macro FAQ

What is the Buffett Indicator?
Popularised by Warren Buffett in 2001, it measures the combined market capitalisation of publicly traded domestic corporations against nominal GDP — which he called “probably the best single measure of where valuations stand at any given moment.”
Why is it currently elevated (218%)?
Highly concentrated capital flows into mega-cap and AI-linked equities have pushed total market value far above domestic output. Use the Numerator toggle in the header to switch between a Wilshire-style total-market proxy (VTI, the default) and the Fed’s Z.1 corporate-equities series (NCBEILQ027S) — the proxy covers financials too and reads a few points higher.
What are the main criticisms?
It compares globalised market caps with domestic GDP. Large US multinationals earn 40%+ of profits overseas — cash flow that lifts valuations but isn’t counted in US GDP, structurally inflating the ratio over time. It also ignores interest rates, which justify higher valuations when low.
Buffett Indicator = Total U.S. Equity Market Capitalisation ÷ Nominal GDP.
